2.3 Sampling Distribution of the Sample Mean

If the population is 1 to 10, we can have population mean and sd

mean(1:10)
## [1] 5.5
sd.p(1:10)
## [1] 2.872281

If we randomly sample 5 record from 1 to 10

s_5<-sample(1:10,5,replace = T)
s_5
## [1] 4 3 7 9 3

The mean and sd of the sampling results (one time)

mean(s_5)
## [1] 5.2
sd(s_5)
## [1] 2.683282

If we sample 5 cases from 1 to 10, how many combinations will have?

allComb<-
  do.call(data.table::CJ, 
          replicate(5, 1:10, simplify = FALSE))
nrow(allComb)
## [1] 100000
head(allComb,10)

The sample mean from each sampling, showing the sample mean from the first 10 sampling results.

rowMeans(allComb)[1:10]
##  [1] 1.0 1.2 1.4 1.6 1.8 2.0 2.2 2.4 2.6 2.8

For the sample mean we have, we can visualize the distribution, calculate mean and sd for the sample mean

hist(rowMeans(allComb))

mean(rowMeans(allComb))
## [1] 5.5
sd.p(rowMeans(allComb))
## [1] 1.284523

Compare population mean/sd with sample mean mean/sd

The population mean = mean of sample mean

mean(1:10)
## [1] 5.5
mean(rowMeans(allComb))
## [1] 5.5

The population sd != sd of sample mean

sd.p(1:10)
## [1] 2.872281
sd.p(rowMeans(allComb))
## [1] 1.284523

But, from the classâ€¦.we know that \[ \sigma _\bar{x} = \sigma / \sqrt{n} \]

We have \[ \sigma / \sqrt{n} \]

sd.p(1:10)/(5^(1/2))
## [1] 1.284523

Then \[ \sigma _\bar{x} \]

sd.p(rowMeans(allComb))
## [1] 1.284523

2.4 Sampling Distribution of the Sample Propotion

To demonstrate the sampling distribution of the sample proportion, we need a demo dataset. We have a population with 100 students. In these 100 students, 40 of them are nearsighted.

NS<-data.frame(StudentID=1:100,
               Nearsighted=c(rep(T,40),rep(F,60)))
NS[sample(1:100,10),]

sum(NS$Nearsighted)
## [1] 40

The population proportion:

sum(NS$Nearsighted)/nrow(NS)
## [1] 0.4

If we sample 40 students and try to understand the sampling destitution of sample proportion.

First of all, we need to check if the sample is large enough and the sample proportions are normally distributed.

40 * (0.4)

Both of the values are greater than 15, so the sampling distribution of sample promotion is normal distribution.
